Singapore

Domestic price transmission and sectoral activity in the Singapore economy

Key Takeaways

From narrative pipeline

Crude oil prices have surged nearly 60 percent since late February, peaking at USD 138 per barrel in early April before moderating to USD 113 by late April. Passthrough to refined products shows significant variation. Jet fuel has experienced the most acute adjustment, rising roughly 92 percent, while bunker fuel has lagged at 56 percent—likely reflecting longer-term contracting and inventory dynamics in marine fuel markets. Gasoline and naphtha have tracked the crude move more closely, with gains of 82 and 99 percent respectively. At the pump, however, passthrough has been substantially dampened. Diesel has posted the highest consumer price increase at 76 percent, but gasoline grades have risen only 20 percent, suggesting retail pricing controls or hedging that shields consumers from the full upstream shock. Notably, electricity tariffs have barely budged at 2 percent despite the crude spike, indicating either lagged cost recovery mechanisms or the dominance of non-oil generation in Singapore's fuel mix.

Petrochemical prices downstream have amplified the crude shock rather than merely passing it through. Southeast Asian ethylene prices have nearly doubled while naphtha—the key feedstock—has risen 99 percent, creating a margin compression dynamic that warrants close monitoring. HDPE has risen 63 percent but China's LLDPE only 25 percent, suggesting regional pricing power differences and possible arbitrage pressures.

Port and air activity data covering March show double-digit growth in container throughput and air freight, likely capturing both pre-war momentum and initial supply-chain repositioning rather than war-driven demand. More granular data on petroleum and petrochemical production indices remains stale at December 2025, leaving a critical information gap on how Singapore's refining and chemicals sectors have actually responded to margin changes and feedstock volatility.
